- Goal: To reflect on the nature of human identity from an Islamic perspective.

  • What does Islam say about our origin and purpose?

**Page 3 – Companion Guide**
- **Summary of Key Points**
  • Humans are unique among creation because we ask deep questions about existence.
  • Our identity is not limited to the physical body; it includes an intellect, a soul, and a purpose.
  • The Islamic concept of fitrah teaches that every human is born with an innate awareness of God and a natural inclination toward truth and goodness.
  • The Quran affirms the dignity of human beings: "We have certainly honored the children of Adam." (17:70)
  • Our ultimate identity is as servants (‘abd) of Allah, and this gives life meaning.

- **Deeper Explanation**
  The question "Who am I?" is perhaps the most fundamental question of human existence. Philosophers, psychologists, and theologians have offered various answers. Some say we are merely biological machines, products of chance. Others define us by our thoughts, our culture, or our achievements. Islam offers a holistic answer: we are created beings, fashioned by a wise and merciful Creator, and we are endowed with a soul that gives us dignity, free will, and moral responsibility.

  The Quran teaches that Allah created Adam with His own hands and breathed into him of His spirit (ruh). This divine breath distinguishes humans from all other creatures. It gives us the capacity for knowledge, love, and moral choice. Yet we are also weak and forgetful; we need guidance. The fitrah is that original, uncorrupted nature that inclines us toward truth. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "Every child is born upon the fitrah, then his parents make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Magian." (Bukhari). So our environment can shape our identity, but deep down, we are seekers of God.

  Understanding that we are servants of Allah is not demeaning; it is liberating. It means our worth is not determined by wealth, status, or appearance, but by our relationship with our Creator. We are here to know Him, love Him, and worship Him. This gives every moment of life significance.

- **Quranic Verses**
  • Surah Al-Isra 17:70 – "And We have certainly honored the children of Adam and carried them on the land and sea and provided for them of the good things and preferred them over much of what We have created, with [definite] preference."
  • Surah Adh-Dhariyat 51:56 – "And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me."
  • Surah At-Tin 95:4 – "We have certainly created man in the best of stature."

- **Hadith References**
  • "Every child is born upon the fitrah; his parents make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Magian." (Sahih al-Bukhari, 1358; Sahih Muslim, 2658)
  • "The strong believer is better and more beloved to Allah than the weak believer, but there is good in both." (Sahih Muslim, 2664) – This hadith reminds us that our identity as believers is dynamic; we can grow in faith and character.

- **Stories/Examples**
  Consider the story of Prophet Adam (peace be upon him). After creating him, Allah commanded the angels to prostrate to him as a sign of respect for this new creation. Iblis (Satan) refused out of arrogance, showing that true identity is not about origin but about humility and obedience. Adam later forgot Allah's command and ate from the forbidden tree, but then he repented and Allah accepted his repentance. This teaches us that humans are fallible but also capable of redemption.

- **Key Takeaways**
  1. Your identity includes body, mind, and soul – you are more than just matter.
  2. You were born with a fitrah – a natural inclination to believe in God.
  3. Your true worth comes from being a servant of Allah, not from worldly measures.
  4. Life’s purpose is to know and worship your Creator.
  5. Honoring your fitrah means seeking truth and living in alignment with your nature.

- **Vocabulary**
  • Fitrah – innate human nature inclined toward God and righteousness.
  • Ruh – soul, spirit; the non‑material essence given by Allah.
  • Nafs – self, ego; can be the lower self (nafs al-ammara) or the peaceful self (nafs al-mutma’innah).
  • ‘Abd – servant, slave (in the sense of devoted worshipper).
  • Khalifah – vicegerent, steward on earth.

## LESSON 1.2: Why Am I Here? The Purpose of Existence

**Page 1 – Lesson Overview**
- Title: Why Am I Here? The Purpose of Existence
- Goal: Understand the Islamic answer to life’s ultimate question.
- Key Questions:
  • What do other philosophies say about purpose?
  • How does the Quran define our purpose?
  • What does "worship" mean in a broad sense?
- Time Estimate: 30 minutes
- Key Terms:
  • ‘Ibadah – worship, service; everything that pleases Allah, done with the right intention.
  • Khalifah – vicegerent, steward on earth.
  • Dunya – this world, the present life.
  • Akhirah – the Hereafter, the eternal life.

**Page 3 – Companion Guide**
- **Summary of Key Points**
  • Purpose is not random or self‑created; it is given by the Creator.
  • The primary purpose of human existence is to know Allah and worship Him.
  • Worship (‘ibadah) encompasses every act done with sincere intention to please Allah, including daily activities like working, eating, and helping others.
  • A secondary purpose is to serve as khalifah – a caretaker of the earth, establishing justice and goodness.
  • Life is a test; our choices determine our outcome in the eternal Hereafter.

- **Deeper Explanation**
  Atheistic existentialism suggests we must create our own meaning in an absurd universe. Other worldviews see purpose in pleasure, power, or social progress. Islam teaches that meaning is not self‑invented but discovered: we are created by a Wise Being who has a plan for us. The Quran states clearly, "I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me" (51:56). Worship here is not limited to rituals; the word ‘ibadah comes from the root ‘abd (servant) and implies a state of humble obedience and love. It includes all thoughts, words, and deeds done with the intention of drawing closer to Allah.

  Additionally, humans are appointed as khalifah (vicegerents) on earth. In Surah Al-Baqarah, Allah tells the angels, "Indeed, I will make upon the earth a successive authority" (2:30). This role involves stewardship: building civilization, maintaining justice, caring for the environment, and helping others. Thus, our purpose has both a vertical dimension (relationship with God) and a horizontal dimension (relationship with creation).

  The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "The world is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever" (Muslim). This means that believers see this life as temporary and sometimes restrictive, because they are always conscious of the Hereafter. The real joy is in the next life. So we live here with purpose, but our ultimate hope is in what comes after.

- **Quranic Verses**
  • Surah Adh-Dhariyat 51:56 – "And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me."
  • Surah Al-Baqarah 2:30 – "And [mention] when your Lord said to the angels, 'Indeed, I will make upon the earth a successive authority.'"
  • Surah Al-Mulk 67:2 – "[He] who created death and life to test you [as to] which of you is best in deed."
  • Surah Al-Anbiya 21:16 – "And We did not create the heaven and the earth and that between them in play."

- **Hadith References**
  • "Verily, Allah does not look at your appearance or your wealth, but He looks at your hearts and deeds." (Sahih Muslim, 2564)
  • "The best of people are those that bring most benefit to the rest of mankind." (Reported by Al-Tabarani, classed as hasan) – This shows that benefiting others is part of worship.

- **Stories/Examples**
  Consider a person who works an honest job to support their family. If they intend to fulfill their responsibility as a provider and please Allah by earning halal income, that work becomes an act of worship. Similarly, a student studying to gain beneficial knowledge that serves the community is engaged in ‘ibadah. This understanding transforms mundane activities into opportunities for spiritual reward.

- **Key Takeaways**
  1. Your ultimate purpose is to worship Allah – this is the foundation of Islamic life.
  2. Worship is comprehensive: every good deed done with sincere intention counts.
  3. You are also a khalifah, entrusted with the care of the earth.
  4. Life is a test; focus on deeds of excellence.
  5. The Hereafter is eternal; invest in it wisely.

- **Vocabulary**
  • ‘Ibadah – worship, service, obedience.
  • Khalifah – vicegerent, successor, steward.
  • Dunya – this lowly world.
  • Akhirah – the last, the Hereafter.
  • Ihsan – excellence, perfection in worship.
